**Q1.** In the circular flow model, if households decide to increase their savings rate from 10% to 15% of their income, ceteris paribus, what would be the immediate impact on the flow of factor payments from firms to households?

A) Factor payments would increase by 5%
B) Factor payments would decrease by 5%
C) Factor payments would remain unchanged in the immediate period
D) Factor payments would increase by 15%

**Correct Answer: C**

**Difficulty: Hard**

**Detailed Solution:**
This question tests understanding of the timing and direction of flows in the circular flow model. Factor payments (wages, rent, interest) are determined by the employment of factors of production (labour, capital, land) and their prices. In the immediate period, these are fixed by existing contracts and employment levels. Households' decision to save more affects how they allocate their disposable income between consumption and savings, but it does not immediately affect their income from factor services. The increased savings would flow into financial markets, potentially affecting investment and future production, but factor payments themselves remain unchanged until employment or wage rates adjust. This highlights the distinction between the income side (factor payments) and the expenditure side (consumption vs. savings) of household activity.

**Key Insight:** Savings decisions affect the allocation of income, not the generation of income itself in the immediate period.

**Q2.** Consider an economy where the government decides to impose a 20% tax on all factor incomes and uses the entire tax revenue to purchase goods and services from domestic firms. Which of the following best describes the net effect on the circular flow?

A) The size of the circular flow decreases because taxes leak out of the system
B) The size of the circular flow increases because government spending injects more than taxes withdraw
C) The size of the circular flow remains unchanged in nominal terms
D) The composition of the circular flow changes from private to public, but the total flow remains unchanged

**Correct Answer: D**

**Difficulty: Hard**

**Detailed Solution:**
This question examines the impact of government's simultaneous role as tax collector and spender. When the government taxes factor incomes, it withdraws (leakage) purchasing power from households. However, when it spends the entire tax revenue on goods and services from firms, it injects (injection) the same amount back into the circular flow. The total nominal value of the circular flow remains unchanged, but its composition changes – what would have been private consumption expenditure becomes government consumption expenditure. This illustrates that government can redirect resources without necessarily changing the total size of the flow. The key insight is that leakages and injections, when equal, leave the total flow unchanged.

**Key Insight:** Government can be a neutral agent in terms of total flow size when taxes equal spending.

**Q3.** In the two-agent circular flow model, if firms decide to increase their investment in new machinery by ₹100 crore and simultaneously households decide to increase their savings by ₹100 crore, what would be the net effect on the equilibrium level of income?

A) Income would increase by ₹100 crore
B) Income would decrease by ₹100 crore
C) Income would remain unchanged
D) Income would increase by an amount determined by the multiplier

**Correct Answer: C**

**Difficulty: Hard**

**Detailed Solution:**
This question tests the concept of leakages and injections in the circular flow. Savings are a leakage from the circular flow (withdrawal of purchasing power), while investment is an injection (addition to purchasing power). When leakages equal injections, the circular flow remains in equilibrium with no tendency for income to change. Here, the increase in savings (leakage) of ₹100 crore exactly matches the increase in investment (injection) of ₹100 crore. Therefore, the equilibrium level of income remains unchanged. Option D would be correct only if the injection occurred without a matching leakage, allowing the multiplier to operate.

**Key Insight:** Equilibrium in the circular flow requires that total leakages (savings, taxes, imports) equal total injections (investment, government spending, exports).

**Q4.** During the COVID-19 lockdown, many urban workers lost their jobs and returned to rural areas, taking up agricultural work. This phenomenon represents which of the following economic adjustments?

A) A movement along the production possibility frontier
B) A shift of factors of production between sectors
C) A decrease in the overall labour force participation rate
D) An increase in the natural rate of unemployment

**Correct Answer: B**

**Difficulty: Hard**

**Detailed Solution:**
This question applies circular flow concepts to real-world events. Workers moving from urban non-office jobs (typically in services or manufacturing) to agricultural work represents a reallocation of the factor of production (labour) from one sector to another. This is a shift of factors between sectors, not a movement along a production possibility frontier (which would involve reallocating resources between two goods within the same economy without changing total resources). Labour force participation may not have decreased (they're still working), and this isn't about unemployment rates (they're employed in agriculture). The circular flow model accommodates such factor mobility between sectors.

**Key Insight:** Economic disruptions can cause factors of production to be reallocated between sectors, changing the composition of output.

**Q5.** Consider an economy where all firms are owned by households, and all households work for firms. If firms decide to retain 40% of their profits for reinvestment instead of distributing them as dividends to households, what is the immediate impact on the circular flow?

A) Household income decreases by 40% of profits, reducing consumption
B) Firm investment increases by 40% of profits, maintaining aggregate expenditure
C) Both A and B occur simultaneously, leaving total expenditure unchanged
D) Neither A nor B occurs because retained earnings are not part of the circular flow

**Correct Answer: C**

**Difficulty: Hard**

**Detailed Solution:**
This question examines the dual nature of retained earnings (undistributed profits). When firms retain profits:
1. From the income side: Households receive less dividend income (40% of profits), which would normally flow to them as factor payments. This is a leakage from the household income stream.
2. From the expenditure side: Firms use these retained earnings for investment (reinvestment), which is an injection into the circular flow.

Both occur simultaneously. The reduction in household income (if they had received dividends) would reduce consumption, but the increase in investment maintains aggregate expenditure. Total expenditure in the economy remains unchanged if the retained earnings are fully invested. This illustrates that corporate saving (retained earnings) is a leakage matched by corporate investment.

**Key Insight:** Retained earnings represent saving by firms, which is a leakage matched by investment injection.

**Q7.** If the price mechanism is described as the "invisible hand" coordinating economic activity, which of the following scenarios best demonstrates this coordination?

A) The government sets minimum wage rates to ensure fair income distribution
B) A shortage of tomatoes leads to higher prices, causing consumers to buy fewer tomatoes and farmers to plant more tomatoes next season
C) A large corporation decides to increase production based on internal sales forecasts
D) Labor unions negotiate higher wages for their members

**Correct Answer: B**

**Difficulty: Medium-Hard**

**Detailed Solution:**
The "invisible hand" refers to the self-organizing nature of markets where prices coordinate the actions of countless individuals without central direction. Option B perfectly illustrates this: a shortage (excess demand) causes prices to rise. The higher price signals two things:
- To consumers: This good is scarce, so reduce consumption (movement along demand curve)
- To producers: This good is profitable, so increase production (future supply response)

This occurs without any central planner directing these adjustments. Options A, C, and D involve conscious human decisions or collective bargaining, not the automatic price mechanism.

**Key Insight:** The invisible hand operates through price signals that simultaneously coordinate the independent decisions of buyers and sellers.

**Q8.** Consider an economy with two agents: households and firms. If households collectively decide to work fewer hours (reduce labour supply) while maintaining the same consumption level by drawing down their past savings, what happens to the circular flow?

A) Production increases because firms must work harder to maintain output
B) Production decreases because less labour is available, but consumption remains constant due to dissaving
C) The circular flow expands because savings are being used
D) Nothing changes because consumption determines production

**Correct Answer: B**

**Difficulty: Hard**

**Detailed Solution:**
This question tests understanding of the relationship between factor supply and production. When households reduce labour supply:
- Firms have less factor input (labour), so production capacity decreases
- With less labour, firms produce less output (real flow from firms to households decreases)
- However, households maintain consumption by dissaving (using past savings)
- This is unsustainable because eventually savings will be exhausted, and consumption will have to adjust to the lower production level

The circular flow shows that in the long run, consumption cannot exceed production. Drawing down savings allows temporary divergence, but the fundamental relationship holds that output determines sustainable consumption.

**Key Insight:** In the circular flow, production (real flow) ultimately constrains sustainable consumption, though savings can temporarily bridge gaps.

**Q14.** If the price of petrol increases significantly, which of the following sequences of events best illustrates the price mechanism at work?

A) Government imposes price controls on petrol → consumers protest → government subsidizes petrol
B) Petrol companies collude to raise prices → consumers have no choice but to pay → companies earn higher profits
C) Higher petrol prices → consumers reduce driving and switch to public transport → demand for cars decreases → car manufacturers reduce production
D) Higher petrol prices → consumers demand wage increases → firms raise product prices → inflation accelerates

**Correct Answer: C**

**Difficulty: Hard**

**Detailed Solution:**
The price mechanism works through a cascade of substitutions and adjustments. Higher petrol prices (a price signal) cause consumers to:
- Reduce quantity demanded of petrol directly (drive less)
- Switch to substitutes (public transport)
- Reduce demand for complements (cars, since cost of using them increased)

Car manufacturers, seeing reduced demand, reduce production. This entire chain occurs through price signals without central direction. Option A involves government intervention, not market mechanism. Option B involves collusion, not competitive market. Option D might occur but is about cost-push inflation, not the coordinating function of prices.

**Key Insight:** The price mechanism coordinates through multiple layers of substitution and complementary adjustments across markets.

**Q18.** If the government increases spending on infrastructure by ₹1,00,000 crore and finances this entirely by borrowing from the public, what is the immediate impact on the circular flow?

A) Both injections and leakages increase by ₹1,00,000 crore, leaving the flow unchanged
B) Injections increase by ₹1,00,000 crore, with no change in leakages, so the flow expands
C) Leakages increase by ₹1,00,000 crore, with no change in injections, so the flow contracts
D) The circular flow is unaffected because borrowing merely transfers existing funds

**Correct Answer: B**

**Difficulty: Hard**

**Detailed Solution:**
When the government borrows from the public, it is transferring existing savings (which are already a leakage) from households to the government. However, the key point is that government spending is an injection. The sequence:
- Households already had savings (leakage)
- Government borrows these savings (no new leakage created)
- Government spends the borrowed funds (injection)
- Net effect: Injections increase by ₹1,00,000 crore, leakages unchanged
- Therefore, the circular flow expands

This is different from taxing, which would be a direct leakage. Borrowing uses existing leakages but doesn't create new ones.

**Key Insight:** Deficit spending financed by borrowing represents a net injection because it puts idle savings back into the circular flow.

**Q31.** In the circular flow model, if households increase their savings rate, ceteris paribus, which of the following must happen for the economy to remain at the same level of output?

A) Investment must increase by an equal amount
B) Government spending must decrease
C) Imports must increase
D) Consumption must increase

**Correct Answer: A**

**Difficulty: Medium**

**Detailed Solution:**

**Key Insight:** Changes in factor supply affect the equilibrium size of the circular flow.

**Q80.** If a country's investment exceeds its domestic savings, what must be true about its trade balance?

A) It must have a trade surplus
B) It must have a trade deficit
C) Its trade balance must be zero
D) No relationship exists

**Correct Answer: B**

**Difficulty: Hard**

**Step-by-Step Solution:**
From national income identity: (S - I) + (T - G) = (X - M)
If I > S, then (S - I) is negative.
For the equation to hold, either (T - G) is positive (government surplus) or (X - M) is negative (trade deficit), or both.
If the government budget is balanced, then (X - M) must be negative → trade deficit.
So investment > savings implies a trade deficit (capital inflow).

**Key Insight:** Excess investment over domestic savings must be financed by foreign capital, which corresponds to a trade deficit.

**Q82.** If the price of a substitute good decreases, what happens to the demand for the original good and to the circular flow for that good?

**Q91.** If the price elasticity of demand for a good is 0.5 (inelastic) and the price increases by 20%, what happens to total revenue and to the circular flow for that good?

A) Revenue increases by 10%, money flow to producers increases
B) Revenue decreases by 10%, money flow to producers decreases
C) Revenue increases by 20%, money flow increases by 20%
D) Revenue decreases by 20%, money flow decreases by 20%

**Correct Answer: A**

**Difficulty: Medium**

**Step-by-Step Solution:**
Elasticity = %ΔQ / %ΔP = 0.5
%ΔQ = 0.5 × 20% = 10% decrease in quantity
Revenue change = (1 + %ΔP) × (1 + %ΔQ) - 1
Approximation: %ΔR ≈ %ΔP + %ΔQ = 20% - 10% = 10% increase
More precisely: (1.2) × (0.9) = 1.08 → 8% increase
So revenue increases, money flow to producers increases.

**Key Insight:** With inelastic demand, price increase increases total revenue.

**Q100.** In the two-agent circular flow model, if households decide to increase their consumption by drawing down their past savings, and firms respond by increasing production to meet the increased demand, what is the net effect on the circular flow in the short run?

A) Income increases, production increases, savings decrease
B) Income increases, production increases, savings increase
C) Income decreases, production decreases, savings increase
D) Income remains unchanged, production adjusts, savings unchanged

**Correct Answer: A**
